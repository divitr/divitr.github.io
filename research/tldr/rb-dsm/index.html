Rao-Blackwellized Score Matching on Manifolds

Figuring out what denoising score matching actually learns when data is drawn from a low-dimensional structure in a high-dimensional space [arXiv]

A lot of modern machine learning assumes the manifold hypothesis: the assumption that the data we care about has some structure to it (formally, this means that it lies on or near some manifold). Under the manifold hypothesis, score matching (a widely used method for learning a data distribution) is fundamentally ill-posed. As a result, practitioners use a variant known as denoising score matching, which resolves the ill-posedness problem, but doesn't necessarily guarantee that we learn the right data distribution. In this work, we examine what exactly is learned by denoising score matching.

Main results.
  • We show that the tangent DSM target has a fundamental singularity on manifolds: the normal-fiber noise variance diverges as d/σ2d/\sigma^2d/σ2 as σ→0+\sigma \to 0^+σ→0+.
  • We show that conditioning on the nearest-point projection π(X)\pi(X)π(X) canonically removes this singularity, giving the unique L2L^2L2-optimal Rao-Blackwellized predictor of the tangent denoising target.
  • We compute the small-noise expansion of this canonical target and show it equals ∇Mlog⁡q\nabla_M \log q∇M​logq plus an explicit σ2\sigma^2σ2 correction decomposing into an intrinsic Tweedie term and an extrinsic curvature term involving the Weingarten and Ricci operators.
  • On SdS^dSd the correction reduces to (1−d/2)∇Mlog⁡q(1-d/2)\nabla_M \log q(1−d/2)∇M​logq; on S2S^2S2 the extrinsic term cancels exactly, though the Tweedie correction remains.

Background

Score Matching

A lot of times, we want to learn a data distribution, but we can't because computing the partition function1 is intractable. So, instead of matching the distribution's values at every point in the domain, we instead match the gradient [Hyv05] (this gets rid of our partition function problem because the partition function is independent of the coordinate xxx). This works fantastically for general data, but if we constrain our data to lie on some low dimensional structure, things fall apart rather quickly. Since the probability distribution is nonzero on the manifold, but zero right off the manifold, we have points at which the derivative does not exist and score matching is not well defined. To get around this, we add noise to the data so that it no longer lies exactly on the manifold.2

Denoising score matching. The practical way to do this is denoising score matching (DSM): we take a clean sample Z∼qZ \sim qZ∼q (here qqq is our data distribution defined on the manifold), corrupt it with Gaussian noise to get X≐Z+σξX \doteq Z + \sigma\xiX≐Z+σξ for ξ∼N(0,ID)\xi \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_D)ξ∼N(0,ID​), and train a network to regress against the denoising direction (Z−X)/σ2(Z-X)/\sigma^2(Z−X)/σ2. By Tweedie's formula [Efr11], minimizing this regression loss recovers the score of the noisy density qσ=q∗ϕσq_\sigma = q * \phi_\sigmaqσ​=q∗ϕσ​, and as σ→0+\sigma \to 0^+σ→0+, this converges to the score of qqq itself. When qqq is a nice density on RD\mathbb R^DRD, we are done: DSM gives you the score up to a σ2\sigma^2σ2 smoothing bias.

But under the manifold hypothesis the ambient score doesn't exist at all (qqq has no density w.r.t. the Lebesgue measure), so there is nothing to take the gradient of. DSM at σ>0\sigma > 0σ>0 is still a well-defined regression problem, it's just not clear what exactly it's estimating. To start thinking about this, it's helpful to split the denoising direction at each noisy point XXX into pieces along and orthogonal to the manifold (denoted MMM from here on out) at the nearest projection π(X)∈M\pi(X) \in Mπ(X)∈M. The normal piece is just (π(X)−X)/σ2(\pi(X) - X)/\sigma^2(π(X)−X)/σ2, a deterministic function of XXX -- so it contains no information about where ZZZ actually came from on MMM. All the signal lives in the tangent piece: Tσ≐1σ2PTπ(X)M(Z−X)∈Tπ(X)M. T_\sigma \doteq \frac{1}{\sigma^2} P_{T_{\pi(X)} M}(Z - X) \in T_{\pi(X)} M. Tσ​≐σ21​PTπ(X)​M​(Z−X)∈Tπ(X)​M.

This leaves a few questions open: what is TσT_\sigmaTσ​ actually estimating? Does it converge to the intrinsic Riemannian score ∇Mlog⁡q\nabla_M \log q∇M​logq? Does it even have a sensible variance as σ→0+\sigma \to 0^+σ→0+?

In order to answer these, we'll need to set up a bit of differential geometry.

Differential Geometry

Differential geometry is concerned with differentiable manifolds: geometric structures that, when we zoom in enough, look like regular flat (Euclidean) space.3 For example, a plane embedded in a three-dimensional space is a manifold. A plane is a special type of manifold since it doesn't have any curvature: most manifolds have two notions of curvature.

Curvature. Extrinsic curvature tells us how MMM bends inside the ambient RD\mathbb{R}^DRD. Intrinsic curvature is inherent to MMM and requires no knowledge of the embedding in ambient space. Both end up as linear maps on the tangent space, so we may compare them directly.

At each z∈Mz \in Mz∈M, the ambient space splits as RD=TzM⊕NzM\mathbb{R}^D = T_z M \oplus N_z MRD=Tz​M⊕Nz​M, with orthogonal projections PTzMP_{T_z M}PTz​M​ and PNzMP_{N_z M}PNz​M​.

Second fundamental form, Weingarten operator, and mean curvature. Consider traveling along MMM in some tangent direction uuu. The velocity vector can't stay constant in RD\mathbb{R}^DRD; the manifold is curving, so uuu must bend. The part of that bending that points normal to MMM is the second fundamental form: IIz(u,v)∈NzMII_z(u, v) \in N_z MIIz​(u,v)∈Nz​M, a symmetric bilinear map on TzM×TzMT_z M \times T_z MTz​M×Tz​M. It's a super clean object for measuring extrinsic curvature since it directly measures how tangent directions get pushed off the manifold. The Weingarten operator is the exact same information, just in the form of a linear map. For each normal direction ν∈NzM\nu \in N_z Mν∈Nz​M, define Wν:TzM→TzMW_\nu : T_z M \to T_z MWν​:Tz​M→Tz​M by ⟨Wνu,v⟩=⟨IIz(u,v),ν⟩\langle W_\nu u, v \rangle = \langle II_z(u, v), \nu \rangle⟨Wν​u,v⟩=⟨IIz​(u,v),ν⟩. WνW_\nuWν​ acts on tangent vectors, so we can apply it directly to things like ∇Mlog⁡q(z)\nabla_M \log q(z)∇M​logq(z) -- which is what shows up in the result. The mean curvature vector is the trace of IIIIII: H(z)≐∑iIIz(ei,ei)∈NzMH(z) \doteq \sum_i II_z(e_i, e_i) \in N_z MH(z)≐∑i​IIz​(ei​,ei​)∈Nz​M for any orthonormal basis (ei)(e_i)(ei​) of TzMT_z MTz​M (the sum is basis-independent). This points in the direction MMM is curving "on average."

Ricci endomorphism. Now, let us turn to intrinsic curvature; ignoring the embedding entirely. If we live on MMM, we can still measure curvature directly by noticing things like how geodesics4 spread apart. The Ricci endomorphism Ricz♯:TzM→TzM\mathrm{Ric}_z^\sharp : T_z M \to T_z MRicz♯​:Tz​M→Tz​M packages this intrinsic curvature into a linear operator on the tangent space.5

Gauss equation. Intrinsic and extrinsic curvature are actually linked: the metric on MMM is induced from the ambient inner product. The exact relationship is given by the Gauss equation Ricz♯=WH(z)−∑αWnα2\mathrm{Ric}_z^\sharp = W_{H(z)} - \sum_\alpha W_{n_\alpha}^2Ricz♯​=WH(z)​−∑α​Wnα​2​, where (nα)(n_\alpha)(nα​) is any orthonormal basis of NzMN_z MNz​M. The takeaway is that intrinsic curvature is determined by the embedding: it's the mean-curvature Weingarten minus a separate "sum of squared Weingartens" contribution.

Rao-Blackwellization and the Nearest-Point Projection

Having established our machinery, we may now return to the question we really care about: what is TσT_\sigmaTσ​ actually estimating, and can we control its variance?

The classical statistics move here is Rao-Blackwellization. The Rao-Blackwell Theorem tells us that conditioning an unbiased estimator on a sufficient statistic can only reduce its variance. Intuitively, we keep the expected value, but integrate out the part of the noise that doesn't carry any signal. The natural thing to condition on in our setting is the nearest-point projection π(X)∈M\pi(X) \in Mπ(X)∈M. It captures the manifold-valued summary of the noisy observation -- "where on MMM did ZZZ likely come from" -- and discards the normal offset, which by construction carries no information about ZZZ. Formally, among all fiber-collapsing summaries S(X)S(X)S(X) -- statistics depending on XXX only through π(X)\pi(X)π(X), i.e. σ(S)⊆σ(π(X))\sigma(S) \subseteq \sigma(\pi(X))σ(S)⊆σ(π(X)) -- the projection π(X)\pi(X)π(X) is the finest. Anything coarser throws away information that π(X)\pi(X)π(X) retains.

Define the Rao-Blackwellized tangent target: rσ(z)≐E[Tσ∣π(X)=z]∈TzMr_\sigma(z) \doteq \mathbb{E}\bigl[T_\sigma \mid \pi(X) = z\bigr] \in T_z Mrσ​(z)≐E[Tσ​∣π(X)=z]∈Tz​M. From the Pythagorean identity for L2L^2L2 projections, we can show that among all functions hhh of π(X)\pi(X)π(X), the choice h(π(X))=rσ(π(X))h(\pi(X)) = r_\sigma(\pi(X))h(π(X))=rσ​(π(X)) minimizes the regression risk: E∣Tσ−h(π(X))∣2=E∣Tσ−rσ(π(X))∣2+E∣rσ(π(X))−h(π(X))∣2. \mathbb{E}|T_\sigma - h(\pi(X))|^2 = \mathbb{E}|T_\sigma - r_\sigma(\pi(X))|^2 + \mathbb{E}|r_\sigma(\pi(X)) - h(\pi(X))|^2. E∣Tσ​−h(π(X))∣2=E∣Tσ​−rσ​(π(X))∣2+E∣rσ​(π(X))−h(π(X))∣2.

Thus, rσr_\sigmarσ​ is the unique (up to null sets) L2L^2L2-optimal target in this class, and the excess risk of any coarser estimator decomposes into the irreducible "noise" part and a separable "estimator error" part. Intuitively, rσr_\sigmarσ​ keeps all the signal-bearing information in TσT_\sigmaTσ​ and averages out the rest.

Variance Collapse

Okay, so we found the L2L^2L2-optimal target, now we want to know if it actually buys us anything quantitatively. The answer is a resounding yes.

The conditional variance of TσT_\sigmaTσ​ given π(X)\pi(X)π(X) is the irreducible portion that can't be removed by any fiber-collapsing summary, and we can write it down exactly:

Theorem 1 (Variance collapse under Rao-Blackwellization). Uniformly in z∈Mz \in Mz∈M, Var⁡(Tσ∣π(X)=z)=dσ2+O(1)\operatorname{Var}\bigl(T_\sigma \mid \pi(X) = z\bigr) = \frac{d}{\sigma^2} + O(1)Var(Tσ​∣π(X)=z)=σ2d​+O(1). Consequently Var⁡(Tσ)=d/σ2+O(1)\operatorname{Var}(T_\sigma) = d/\sigma^2 + O(1)Var(Tσ​)=d/σ2+O(1), while Var⁡(rσ(π(X)))=O(1)\operatorname{Var}(r_\sigma(\pi(X))) = O(1)Var(rσ​(π(X)))=O(1).

The intuition for the d/σ2d/\sigma^2d/σ2 is pretty straightforward. Conditioned on π(X)=z\pi(X) = zπ(X)=z, the latent ZZZ is concentrated on MMM within a tangent ball of radius ∼σ\sim \sigma∼σ around zzz (this is just the posterior of ZZZ given XXX). So Z−XZ - XZ−X has typical tangent magnitude ∼σ\sim \sigma∼σ, and dividing by σ2\sigma^2σ2 gives a typical magnitude of 1/σ1/\sigma1/σ. Squaring and summing over the ddd tangent dimensions gives d/σ2d/\sigma^2d/σ2. So, as we shrink the noise, the variance of TσT_\sigmaTσ​ grows, which is exactly the opposite of what we want from a regression target.

The d/σ2d/\sigma^2d/σ2 floor isn't just a property of our particular estimator -- it's an irreducible Bayes-risk floor for any fiber-collapsing summary. That is, for any statistic S(X)S(X)S(X) with σ(S)⊆σ(π(X))\sigma(S) \subseteq \sigma(\pi(X))σ(S)⊆σ(π(X)), the best possible L2L^2L2 predictor of TσT_\sigmaTσ​ based on SSS has expected squared error at least d/σ2+O(1)d/\sigma^2 + O(1)d/σ2+O(1). Coarsening π(X)\pi(X)π(X) doesn't help; only the projection itself achieves the floor exactly.

σ2\sigma^2σ2 Correction

Now that we have a target with bounded variance, the natural next question is what it actually equals. Expanding rσ(z)r_\sigma(z)rσ​(z) as a power series in σ\sigmaσ: rσ(z)=∇Mlog⁡q(z)+σ2[bq(z)+gMext(z)]+o(σ2), r_\sigma(z) = \nabla_M \log q(z) + \sigma^2 \bigl[b_q(z) + g_M^{\text{ext}}(z)\bigr] + o(\sigma^2), rσ​(z)=∇M​logq(z)+σ2[bq​(z)+gMext​(z)]+o(σ2), uniformly on MMM.

The leading term is the intrinsic Riemannian score ∇Mlog⁡q\nabla_M \log q∇M​logq -- exactly what fully-intrinsic methods regress against. So at leading order, ambient DSM (after the Rao-Blackwellization fix) recovers the right thing. The σ2\sigma^2σ2 correction is more interesting. It splits into two distinct pieces:

  1. The intrinsic Tweedie bias bq(z)=12∇M(ΔMlog⁡q+∣∇Mlog⁡q∣2)b_q(z) = \frac{1}{2} \nabla_M\left(\Delta_M \log q + |\nabla_M \log q|^2\right)bq​(z)=21​∇M​(ΔM​logq+∣∇M​logq∣2). This is the manifold analog of the flat-Tweedie bias: it comes from Gaussian smoothing of the density and would appear even on a flat support. It depends on qqq but not on the embedding, and it's the same correction you'd get from intrinsic methods.
  2. The extrinsic curvature term gMext(z)=(12WH(z)−Ricz♯)∇Mlog⁡q(z)g_M^{\text{ext}}(z) = \bigl(\tfrac{1}{2} W_{H(z)} - \mathrm{Ric}_z^\sharp\bigr)\nabla_M \log q(z)gMext​(z)=(21​WH(z)​−Ricz♯​)∇M​logq(z). This piece matters for non-flat manifolds. It depends on the embedding of MMM in RD\mathbb{R}^DRD via the Weingarten and Ricci operators defined before. It's invisible to any method that corrupts ZZZ by intrinsic Riemannian noise -- intrinsic noise never leaves the manifold, and thus never feels the embedding.

A nice property of gMextg_M^{\text{ext}}gMext​ is that the operator 12WH−Ric♯\tfrac{1}{2} W_H - \mathrm{Ric}^\sharp21​WH​−Ric♯ is purely geometric -- it depends only on how MMM sits in RD\mathbb{R}^DRD, not on qqq. So if we've trained a score model s^(z)≈∇Mlog⁡q(z)\hat s(z) \approx \nabla_M \log q(z)s^(z)≈∇M​logq(z), we can apply this operator to our model's output and subtract σ2\sigma^2σ2 times the result. The correction is fully post-hoc; you don't need to re-train, and you don't need to know qqq.6

  • d=1d = 1d=1: α1=+1/2\alpha_1 = +1/2α1​=+1/2;

On S2S^2S2, the extrinsic curvature correction vanishes identically, and ambient DSM recovers the intrinsic score up to only the flat-Tweedie bias bqb_qbq​.

It's worth emphasizing that this is a coincidence specific to the round sphere, not a general 2-manifold property. The torus T2⊂R3T^2 \subset \mathbb{R}^3T2⊂R3 is intrinsically flat (Ric♯=0\mathrm{Ric}^\sharp = 0Ric♯=0) but extrinsically curved, and the corresponding gT2extg_{T^2}^{\text{ext}}gT2ext​ has coefficient +1/2+1/2+1/2 -- a measurable embedding-only bias. Higher-dimensional spheres also show the bias clearly: on S6S^6S6 and S10S^{10}S10, raw ambient DSM under-concentrates the equilibrium distribution by about 17% and 33% respectively, both removed by the post-hoc correction.

This explains a longstanding empirical observation: ambient diffusion models work surprisingly well on most test cases such as climate or weather data, which naturally live on S2S^2S2.
