Goal

This is not meant to be a new theorem about μ\muμP. I just thought it was interesting to consider a geometric "selection principle": among all width-transferable scalings, pick the one with maximal non-degenerate Wasserstein transport. This reframes μ\muμP as a critical-mobility point selected by a constrained variational problem, which is (maybe?) portable to other parameter spaces and metrics.

In this view, the standard "lazy" (kernel-like) parameterization fails because the transport distance is zero (the particles freeze). Unstable parameterizations fail because the transport distance explodes. μ\muμP emerges as the precise scaling regime where the Wasserstein speed of the layers is non-zero, finite, and coupled.

We will proceed in two stages:

  1. Warm-Up. We analyze a 1-hidden-layer MLP with biases under square loss. We explicitly compute the Wasserstein speed of the neuron measure and show that μ\muμP is the only scaling that allows non-degenerate transport.
  2. Generalization. We lift this logic to a deep MLP. We formulate a "Total Transport" objective -- summing the Wasserstein path lengths across all layers -- and show that maximizing this quantity forces every hidden layer to adopt the specific n\sqrt{n}n​ scaling factors characteristic of μ\muμP.

Networks as Particle Systems

We shall treat the network as a dynamic system of interacting particles.

Model

We focus on a standard 1-hidden-layer (MLP) with width nnn with biases. For conceptual clarity in the OT setting, we use mean-field normalization:

fn(x)=1n∑i=1naiσ(wi⊤x+bi)+c f_n(x) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n a_i \sigma(w_i^\top x + b_i) + c fn​(x)=n1​i=1∑n​ai​σ(wi⊤​x+bi​)+c

(Note: Standard μ\muμP uses 1n\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}n​1​ normalization. The two formulations are related by rescaling aaa and time. The mean-field form aligns perfectly with OT geometry where measures are probability distributions.)

Here, the "particles" are the hidden neurons. Each neuron iii is defined by its parameter tuple θi\theta_iθi​:

where aia_iai​ is the output weight, wiw_iwi​ is the input weight vector, and bib_ibi​ is the bias. The scalar ccc is the global output bias.

This is exactly the mean-field limit: the network function is an integral against the neuron distribution.

Dynamics and Wasserstein Speed

We model training as gradient flow (continuous-time gradient descent). The parameters evolve according to:

The core quantity we care about is the Wasserstein kinetic energy. In Optimal Transport geometry (specifically the W2W_2W2​ metric), the instantaneous kinetic energy of the measure μt\mu_tμt​ is:

Ekin(μt)=12∫∥vt(θ)∥2dμt(θ)≈12n∑i=1n∥θ˙i∥2 E_{\text{kin}}(\mu_t) = \frac{1}{2} \int \left\| v_t(\theta) \right\|^2 d\mu_t(\theta) \approx \frac{1}{2n} \sum_{i=1}^n \left\| \dot{\theta}_i \right\|^2 Ekin​(μt​)=21​∫∥vt​(θ)∥2dμt​(θ)≈2n1​i=1∑n​​θ˙i​​2

Speed(μt)=(1n∑i=1n∥θ˙i∥2)1/2=2Ekin(μt) \text{Speed}(\mu_t) = \left( \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \left\| \dot{\theta}_i \right\|^2 \right)^{1/2} = \sqrt{2 E_{\text{kin}}(\mu_t)} Speed(μt​)=(n1​i=1∑n​​θ˙i​​2)1/2=2Ekin​(μt​)​

This quantity dictates how the system behaves:

  • If Speed =0= 0=0, kinetic energy vanishes and the system is frozen (lazy, kernel-like regime).
  • If Speed =∞= \infty=∞, kinetic energy diverges and the system explodes (Unstable regime).
  • If Speed =O(1)= O(1)=O(1), kinetic energy is finite and nonzero—the system flows effectively (Feature Learning / μ\muμP).

This is exactly a phase transition story: μ\muμP sits at the critical point where kinetic energy is O(1)O(1)O(1).

Learning Rates as Mobilities

In physics, the relationship between force and velocity is mediated by a mobility tensor. Here, learning rates play the role of mobilities (inverse friction coefficients). We write the dynamics as:

θ˙i=−M(n)∇θiL(θ) \dot{\theta}_i = -M(n) \nabla_{\theta_i} \mathcal{L}(\theta) θ˙i​=−M(n)∇θi​​L(θ)

where M(n)M(n)M(n) is a block-diagonal mobility tensor that scales with width. The choice of scaling for M(n)M(n)M(n) determines the mobility scale: too small (lazy regime) and particles freeze; too large and particles accelerate infinitely. μ\muμP corresponds to the critical mobility where kinetic energy stays finite but nonzero.

Learning rates act like mobilities (inverse friction coefficients) in an overdamped Langevin system: force is −∇L-\nabla\mathcal{L}−∇L, velocity is θ˙\dot{\theta}θ˙, and AT\mathcal{A}_TAT​ measures total dissipation.

Critical Scaling

The Wasserstein speed depends on the maximum velocity across all parameter types:

Speed(μt)=O(nmax⁡(γa,γw,γb)−1) \text{Speed}(\mu_t) = O\left(n^{\max(\gamma_a, \gamma_w, \gamma_b) - 1}\right) Speed(μt​)=O(nmax(γa​,γw​,γb​)−1)

This gives us three regimes:

  • Subcritical. max⁡(γa,γw,γb)<1  ⟹  \max(\gamma_a, \gamma_w, \gamma_b) \lt 1 \impliesmax(γa​,γw​,γb​)<1⟹ Speed →0\to 0→0 as n→∞n \to \inftyn→∞. The neurons barely move; this is the lazy (kernel-like) regime.
  • Critical. max⁡(γa,γw,γb)=1  ⟹  \max(\gamma_a, \gamma_w, \gamma_b) = 1 \impliesmax(γa​,γw​,γb​)=1⟹ Speed =O(1)= O(1)=O(1). The neurons move at a non-degenerate rate; this is feature learning.
  • Supercritical. max⁡(γa,γw,γb)>1\max(\gamma_a, \gamma_w, \gamma_b) > 1max(γa​,γw​,γb​)>1 \implies Speed →∞\to \infty→∞ as n→∞n \to \inftyn→∞. The dynamics explode and width-transferability breaks.

For the output bias ccc, the critical scaling is γc=0\gamma_c = 0γc​=0 since its gradient is already O(1)O(1)O(1).

Selection Principle: Critical Mobility

Width-transferability requires that the training trajectory in function space converges as n→∞n \to \inftyn→∞. Empirically and theoretically, this rules out the supercritical regime. So DT=0\mathcal{D}_T = 0DT​=0 (the transferability constraint) effectively enforces:

max⁡(γa,γw,γb)≤1,γc≤0 \max(\gamma_a, \gamma_w, \gamma_b) \le 1, \qquad \gamma_c \le 0 max(γa​,γw​,γb​)≤1,γc​≤0

To maximize feature learning (non-zero transport), we want to maximize the Wasserstein speed subject to this constraint. This forces us to the boundary:

γa=γw=γb=1,γc=0 \boxed{\gamma_a = \gamma_w = \gamma_b = 1, \qquad \gamma_c = 0} γa​=γw​=γb​=1,γc​=0​

This corresponds to the μ\muμP scaling: hidden-unit parameters get learning rates that scale as nnn, while the output parameters use constant learning rates. Uniqueness is meant within the family of power-law learning-rate scalings (mobilities) considered here, modulo an overall rescaling of time.

Deep μ\muμP Result (Scaling Argument)

For deep networks, rigorous OT gradient-flow limits are less clean than in the two-layer case. The following is a somewhat handwavy scaling argument that relies on the following assumptions: signals remain O(1)O(1)O(1), no gradient explosion, and approximate exchangeability across neurons.

If we assign learning rates as:

ηWℓ(n)=ηℓ,0nγW,ηbℓ(n)=ηb,0nγb,ηw(n)=ηw,0nγout,ηc(n)=ηc,0nγc \eta_{W^\ell}(n) = \eta_{\ell,0} n^{\gamma_W}, \quad \eta_{b^\ell}(n) = \eta_{b,0} n^{\gamma_b}, \quad \eta_w(n) = \eta_{w,0} n^{\gamma_{\text{out}}}, \quad \eta_c(n) = \eta_{c,0} n^{\gamma_c} ηWℓ​(n)=ηℓ,0​nγW​,ηbℓ​(n)=ηb,0​nγb​,ηw​(n)=ηw,0​nγout​,ηc​(n)=ηc,0​nγc​

then each hidden layer has the same critical exponent 12\tfrac{1}{2}21​. The same vanish/explode argument applies:

  • If max⁡(γW,γb)<12\max(\gamma_W, \gamma_b) \lt \tfrac{1}{2}max(γW​,γb​)<21​, then ∥μ˙tℓ,n∥W2→0\left\| \dot{\mu}^{\ell,n}_t \right\|_{W_2} \to 0​μ˙​tℓ,n​​W2​​→0 and that layer freezes (lazy).
  • If max⁡(γW,γb)>12\max(\gamma_W, \gamma_b) > \tfrac{1}{2}max(γW​,γb​)>21​, then ∥μ˙tℓ,n∥W2→∞\left\| \dot{\mu}^{\ell,n}_t \right\|_{W_2} \to \infty​μ˙​tℓ,n​​W2​​→∞ and width-transferability breaks.
  • The boundary max⁡(γW,γb)=12\max(\gamma_W, \gamma_b) = \tfrac{1}{2}max(γW​,γb​)=21​ gives non-degenerate transport.

The mechanism is clearest at the preactivation level. For a typical neuron iii at layer ℓ\ellℓ:
